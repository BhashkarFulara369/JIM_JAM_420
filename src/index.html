OSD-379
Research Overview
In order to gain a better understanding of the mechanisms involved in metabolic disorders in response to microgravity, it is important to study the somatotrophic function of the pituitary gland controlling growth and anabolic processes in mammals. Previous inflight experiments have shown a progressive minimization of endocrine regulatory function and inhibition of GH production and secretion with increased flight time. The objective of this study was to determine the histological and cytokaryometric changes in the somatotroph cells of rats flown on SLS-2.'

OSD-665
Research Overview
The objective of the Rodent Research-23 mission (RR-23) was to better understand the effects of spaceflight on the eyes, specifically on the structure and function of the arteries, veins, and lymphatic vessels that are needed to maintain vision. To this end, twenty male, C57BL/6J, 16-17 weeks-old mice were delivered to the International Space Station (ISS) on SpaceX-21 in a single transporter, transferred to two rodent habitats, and maintained in microgravity for 38 days. Flight mice were then returned to Earth alive (January 13th, 2021). Right extensor digitorum longus muscle samples were preserved by immersion in liquid nitrogen and stored at -80C until RNA was extracted, and libraries generated and sequenced (target 60 M clusters per sample, PE 150 bp). This dataset features 9 samples from the Flight group, 9 samples from the Habitat Ground Control group, and 9 samples from the Vivarium Ground Control group.
Methodology
The methodology for NASA OSD-665 involved a structured and systematic approach to ensure the project adhered to NASA's standards. The process began with thorough planning and requirements gathering, where project goals were defined, and functional and technical requirements were established through collaboration among stakeholders. Following this, a detailed system design was developed, incorporating advanced hardware and software components to meet the mission's objectives. Prototypes were then created and tested under simulated conditions, allowing for iterative improvements based on the test outcomes. Data collection was carried out using sensors and simulations, with computational models used to analyze and refine the system. Extensive validation and verification procedures were conducted to ensure the system met operational and safety standards.
About Our Project :-
Biological experiments conducted in space are at the forefront of scientific discovery, offering unique insights that are unattainable on Earth. These experiments, however, come with a host of challenges. They must be meticulously prepared, loaded into rockets, launched into the harsh environment of space, performed using specialized hardware, and often returned to Earth for detailed analysis. This intricate process can be a significant barrier to the widespread use and understanding of the valuable data generated. Our project aims to bridge this gap by creating a tool that generates informative and compelling visualizations of biological experiments performed in space. By transforming complex data into accessible and engaging visuals, we hope to democratize access to this information, enabling scientists, educators, and the general public to better understand and appreciate the groundbreaking work being done beyond our planet.
Our Mission :-
Our mission is to simplify the complexity of space-based biological experiments through innovative visualization techniques. We believe that by making this data more accessible, we can inspire a new generation of scientists and enthusiasts, foster greater collaboration across disciplines, and accelerate the pace of discovery.
